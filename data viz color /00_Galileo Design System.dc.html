04 — Observed design cases
Case 1

Several Spend series use orange, light orange, and dark orange. Combinations look distinct as swatches but become harder to distinguish as thin lines.

How should related series preserve metric identity while remaining clearly distinguishable?
Case 2

Wide bars and dense fills may visually dominate thinner lines. Visual weight is unbalanced across mark types.

How should hierarchy be balanced across different marks in combination charts?
Case 3

A chart with 9–10 visible values across metrics, scenarios, methods, and value types. Color, tone, pattern, and style become difficult to decode.

05 — Design principles
01
Start with the primary comparison
Before choosing color or style, identify what the user must compare first: metrics, dimension members, scenarios, or value types.
02
Color represents one primary distinction
Use hue to identify the most important comparison. Avoid assigning several unrelated meanings to color simultaneously.
03
Secondary meaning uses the appropriate channel
Different marks support different treatments: lines (dash, stroke weight, markers), bars (fill, outline, pattern), areas (opacity, outline), points (shape, fill).
04
Simplify structure before adding encoding
When a chart requires many colors, tones, and patterns, reconsider filtering, small multiples, progressive disclosure, or separate focused views.

07 — Quick design check
  • Can users identify the primary comparison quickly?
  • Is color carrying more than one unrelated meaning?
  • Are related series distinguishable at the real mark size?
  • Does the treatment work for the specific mark type?
  • Are patterns, tones, or opacity improving clarity?
  • Is the legend explaining rather than rescuing the chart?
  • Should the chart be simplified before adding encoding?
